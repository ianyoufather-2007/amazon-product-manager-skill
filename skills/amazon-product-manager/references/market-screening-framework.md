# Market Screening Framework

Use this reference before product selection. The goal is to choose the right market pool, not just a single product. This is especially important for small brands that need a repeatable product line, not one lucky ASIN.

This framework is inspired by publicly observable large consumer-hardware brand behavior such as Anker's focus on coherent product arenas like charging/energy storage, smart home, and smart audio/video. Treat this as an external abstraction, not a claim about any company's internal process.

## Market Screening Principle

Screen markets in this order:

1. Macro arena: broad demand space such as charging, home office, kitchen prep, pet travel, baby safety, home organization, outdoor power, or sleep.
2. Scenario cluster: specific use cases such as travel charging, desk charging, camping power, apartment security, small-space storage, or meal prep.
3. Product line: a family of products that can share audience, brand promise, supply chain, content, and keywords.
4. Entry wedge: the first SKU or bundle a small brand can realistically launch.
5. Expansion path: follow-on SKUs, accessories, bundles, upgrades, or premium versions.

Do not start with an isolated SKU when the user is asking a strategy question. Start with the market pool and only then recommend products.

## Large-Brand Style, Small-Brand Fit

Large brands tend to build around durable arenas, technology/platform leverage, portfolio coherence, and brand trust. Small brands should borrow the discipline but narrow the execution:

- Choose a market with multiple SKU extensions, but launch one wedge first.
- Prefer a narrow scenario where large competitors are broad or generic.
- Avoid markets where winning requires patents, heavy R&D, certification depth, massive review volume, or large launch budgets.
- Prefer markets where content, bundling, design, packaging, or underserved user segments can create a defensible difference.
- Require each market to have a plausible second and third product before treating it as a strategic lane.

## Market Pool Score

Score each market from 1 to 5.

| Factor | 1 | 3 | 5 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Demand durability | Fad, seasonal spike, weak repeat evidence | Stable but narrow demand | Durable multi-year need with many search paths |
| Keyword cluster depth | One or two thin terms | Several related terms | Deep cluster across use cases, modifiers, and buyer segments |
| Segment clarity | Everyone/commodity | Some identifiable segments | Clear buyer segments and scenarios |
| Price-band health | Race to bottom | Mixed price bands | Premium or mid-premium room with visible value drivers |
| Competitive openings | Head brands dominate every angle | Some weak listings or underserved niches | Clear gaps in content, design, bundle, feature, or audience |
| Review barrier | Winners have huge reviews and high ratings | Mixed review barriers | Newer sellers can still rank or convert |
| Margin potential | Hard to exceed 15% net margin | 15-25% possible | 25%+ net margin possible after ads/returns |
| Operational fit | High compliance, defect, MOQ, or support risk | Manageable complexity | Simple supply, small/light FBA, controllable QA |
| Capital fit | Large MOQ, slow turn, high cash risk | Moderate cash need | Small test batch and fast inventory turn possible |
| Portfolio extensibility | One-off SKU | Some adjacent products | Clear product ladder, accessories, bundles, or upgrades |

Interpretation:

- 40-50: priority market candidate.
- 32-39: promising but needs wedge validation.
- 24-31: watch or test only with a very narrow wedge.
- Below 24: avoid for a small brand.

## Anker-Style Market Questions

Use these questions to pressure-test whether the market behaves like a strategic lane:

- Is there a durable user job, not just a trend keyword?
- Can the brand own a recognizable promise in this lane?
- Can one capability, supplier base, design language, or content system support multiple SKUs?
- Can the first product create permission for the second and third product?
- Is there a premiumization path through better design, reliability, convenience, portability, safety, ecosystem fit, or bundled experience?
- Can the product line survive Amazon copycats through brand trust, quality, content, or iteration speed?
- Can a small brand enter through a niche segment before competing with category leaders?

## Small-Brand Reject Rules

Avoid or downgrade a market when several of these are true:

- Top sellers have high ratings, huge review counts, strong brand search, and aggressive pricing.
- The product requires deep certification, safety liability, batteries, electronics QA, or regulated claims beyond the team's capability.
- The price band is low and shipping/FBA fees consume the margin.
- Differentiation is mostly cosmetic and easy to copy.
- The market requires heavy influencer spend or paid ads before organic conversion exists.
- The MOQ or lead time makes testing too expensive.
- The first SKU has no credible follow-on products.

## Market Screening Output

Use this structure:

1. Market decision: `Enter Market`, `Watch Market`, or `Avoid Market`.
2. Market thesis: one sentence describing the buyer, scenario, pain, product lane, and business upside.
3. Market score: table across the 10 factors above.
4. Best wedge: first SKU/bundle to test, target price band, and target segment.
5. Expansion path: second and third products if the wedge works.
6. Evidence to collect: keywords, competitors, reviews, price band, costs, compliance, and supplier feasibility.
7. Small-brand risks: capital, reviews, ads, compliance, supply, or copycat risk.
8. Next action: 7-day research plan or 30-day validation plan.

## Market Funnel

When comparing many ideas, filter them in this order:

1. Remove markets with obvious compliance, liability, or capital mismatch.
2. Remove markets with weak keyword cluster depth or no durable use case.
3. Remove markets with low price-band health and poor net margin potential.
4. Remove markets where top competitors have overwhelming review and brand moats.
5. Keep markets with clear scenario clusters, reachable wedges, and product-line extensibility.
6. Rank finalists by small-brand fit and validation speed.
